# THE RECRUITER'S SHORTLIST
### A curated study list for Hemanth — from emmabostian/developer-portfolios (1,820 entries) and the canon it's missing

**How this list was built, honestly:** I cross-referenced the full 1,820-entry
list against portfolios with genuine, verifiable reputations. Tier A entries
are ON that list. Tier B are canonical references the list *doesn't* contain
but that matter more for your specific project than 90% of what's on it.
I will not pad to a round 50 with names I can't vouch for — a recruiter's
shortlist beats a hoarder's spreadsheet. Caveat: sites get redesigned; a few
notes below may describe an earlier version. Every entry includes *what to
steal for The Living Pipeline* — study these, never clone them.

## TIER A — On the list, and genuinely worth your time (9)

**1. Brittany Chiang — brittanychiang.com**
The most-cloned developer portfolio in history. Study it to understand *why*
it works — ruthless hierarchy, one accent color, experience-first — and then
understand that because 10,000 people cloned it, resembling it is now a
negative signal. Your anti-template instinct is correct.

**2. Lee Robinson — leerob.io**
The writing-as-portfolio model. His site is thin; his essays on engineering
and career are the asset. This is the strongest existing validation of your
`/engineering` page: recruiters at serious companies read his posts, not his
homepage. Steal: dated, versioned writing; zero decoration.

**3. Dustin Brett — dustinbrett.com**
A full OS running in the browser — the reference for "portfolio as working
software." Two lessons: (a) working software is unforgettable, exactly your
Moment thesis; (b) he *owns* the OS genre now, which is why the panel killed
your OS-adjacent ideas. Steal the principle, never the form.

**4. Paco (Francisco Salido) — paco.fyi**
Extreme minimalism with obsessive micro-craft. Proof that restraint reads as
seniority. Steal: how few elements a page can hold and still feel designed.
Your 2D fallback should aspire to this.

**5. Delba — delba.dev**
The best visual-engineering explainers in the industry (she does this
professionally for Vercel). Her post layouts — progressive disclosure,
inline interactive figures — are the blueprint for your SecureRAG case-study
pages. Steal: diagrams that build up stepwise as you read.

**6. Adham Dannaway — adhamdannaway.com**
The famous split-face "I design / I code" hero. A decade old and still cited
— because it states a dual identity in one image, instantly. Your equivalent
one-glance claim is "AI × Security × evidence." Steal the compression, not
the split.

**7. Keita Yamada — p5aholic.me**
Creative WebGL with Japanese restraint — near-monochrome, tiny type, motion
so quiet it whispers. The counter-reference to Awwwards noise. Steal: how 3D
feels premium when it's *underlit*, not overlit. Directly relevant to your
fog-and-hairline aesthetic.

**8. Nikita Sobolev — sobolevn.me**
Almost brutally plain: OSS work, talks, commits — pure evidence, zero
theater. The extreme end of your proof-over-theater axis. Steal: the
confidence of letting a contributions list *be* the design.

**9. Stuart Blackler — codewithstu.tv**
Principal-engineer positioning done through content (AWS/.NET). Worth 10
minutes for how a senior person converts teaching into credibility — the
tone your ADRs should have: calm, specific, unhurried.

## TIER C — How to mine the other 1,780 yourself (the recruiter's filter)

Since the list tags roles, grep it like a dataset (you have the skills):
pull entries tagged `[AI Engineer]`, `[Security]`, `3D`, or `Animated`, and
run each through this **30-second recruiter test** — it's the exact test
your own site will face:

1. **5 seconds:** Do I know who you are and what you claim? (Name, role,
   one-line claim above the fold.)
2. **15 seconds:** Is there evidence one click away? (A real project with a
   real link — not a modal, not a PDF, not "coming soon.")
3. **30 seconds:** Did anything make me *feel* competence? (One craft
   detail, one working thing, one sharp sentence.)

**Instant red flags (also your anti-checklist):** progress-bar skills ·
"passionate about technology" bios · project cards linking to tutorials
they followed · stock 3-column feature grids · a résumé button that doesn't
work · broken dark mode · Lighthouse-visible jank on first scroll.

**The uncomfortable recruiter truth:** of 1,820 entries, well under 5% pass
the 30-second test, and *that* is your real competition math. You don't need
to beat Bruno Simon. You need to be unmistakably in the top 1% of the pile
an actual recruiter sees — and a working pipeline plus an `/engineering`
page already puts you somewhere none of the 1,820 currently stand.
